## 20260712 1520MT - Three Candidate Families

The user asked for a short comparison of the three candidates.

### v1a: Clay Millennium Prize Problems

Best use:

- hallucination discipline,
- overclaim detection,
- formal proof-boundary testing.

Strength:

- extremely clear validation standard,
- high public recognition,
- $1M prize structure,
- strong test for whether AI fabricates proof-like claims.

Weakness:

- likely too abstract for the first public case study unless framed as
  "watch AI analyze why it cannot solve this."

Likely candidates:

- P versus NP,
- Riemann hypothesis,
- Navier-Stokes existence and smoothness.

Official source:

https://www.claymath.org/millennium-problems/

### v1b: XPRIZE-Style Challenges

Best use:

- practical public-facing case study,
- operational planning,
- measurement and deployment analysis.

Strength:

- concrete,
- measurable,
- public-readable,
- naturally suited to staged plans and failure-mode analysis.

Weakness:

- AI may drift into generic "innovation" language unless held to the official
  prize rules and metrics.

Likely candidates:

- Water Scarcity,
- Wildfire,
- Healthspan,
- Quantum Applications.

Official source:

https://www.xprize.org/competitions

### v1c: Other Formal Cash-Reward / Prize Problems

Best use:

- flexible case selection,
- potentially manageable formal rules,
- broader search beyond Clay and XPRIZE.

Strength:

- may produce a better-sized first case if a current, legitimate, clearly
  validated problem is found.

Weakness:

- requires more source verification,
- may include unsuitable categories,
- must avoid coercive, unsafe, unlawful, or resource-inappropriate targets.

Potential sources:

- government prize competitions,
- scientific challenges,
- cryptography/security challenges,
- medical or engineering competitions,
- other formal challenge platforms.

Note:

Challenge.gov reported that its platform was sunset on 2026-03-30 and now
points public innovators to USA.gov resources.

Source:

https://www.challenge.gov/
